\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Agriculture remains the backbone of rural economies in Sub-Saharan Africa, employing more than 60 percent of the labour force and constituting the primary source of income for the majority of rural households. At the same time, the region continues to experience recurrent episodes of violent conflict---civil wars, communal violence, insurgency, and organised crime---that impose enormous costs on welfare and development. Over the past two decades, both agricultural productivity and conflict patterns have been shaped by intensifying climatic variability, raising pressing questions about the causal links between crop outcomes and violence.

Whether and how agricultural conditions affect conflict is theoretically ambiguous. On one side, the opportunity-cost hypothesis predicts that higher yields raise the returns to peaceful production, thereby increasing the cost of participation in violence and reducing conflict. On the other, the predation hypothesis holds that agricultural abundance creates contestable surplus that invites appropriation under conditions of weak property rights and limited state capacity, potentially increasing violence. Separating these mechanisms empirically requires measuring the agricultural shock precisely and credibly isolating its causal effect on conflict.

The existing empirical literature has made substantial progress, but faces a central identification challenge: both the sign and magnitude of the agriculture--conflict relationship depend critically on how agricultural conditions are measured and whether the estimation strategy credibly addresses endogeneity. Most studies rely on climate variables---rainfall and temperature---as proxies for agricultural shocks, exploiting their exogenous variation to estimate intent-to-treat effects. While this approach avoids reverse-causality concerns, it conflates the direct effects of climate on conflict (through health, heat stress, migration, and other channels) with agricultural productivity effects, and imposes strong homogeneity assumptions given that the same weather shock produces very different yield outcomes depending on crop choice, irrigation, and local adaptation.

This paper takes a different approach. Rather than using climate variables as proxies, I directly measure predicted crop yields at fine spatial and temporal resolution using satellite-based machine-learning models. Specifically, I train XGBoost models on plot-level yield observations from LSMS-ISA household surveys, using features constructed from vegetation indices (EVI, NDVI, GCVI), temperature (including growing and killing degree days), and environmental lags aligned to each location's mode harvest month. These models generate predicted yields for every Admin-2 district in six Sub-Saharan African countries across every year from 2010 to 2024. Because the predictions are constructed entirely from exogenous environmental inputs, they are not mechanically contaminated by local conflict outcomes---addressing a key concern about the endogeneity of observed yields.

I merge these yield predictions with geocoded conflict event data from ACLED and construct a balanced Admin-2 $\times$ year panel comprising 13,890 observations across 926 districts. The preferred outcome measure is the count of ACLED-recorded conflict events within a 3-month forward window following harvest, with 6-month and 12-month windows as robustness checks. All specifications include Admin-2 fixed effects (absorbing time-invariant geography, ethnic composition, and institutions) and year fixed effects (absorbing common shocks including global commodity prices and pandemic-era disruptions), with standard errors clustered at the Admin-2 level.

The main findings are as follows. Across the preferred linear two-way fixed-effects specifications---including both OLS and Poisson count models---predicted yield has no statistically significant effect on the 3-month forward conflict count. This null result is consistent across alternative conflict windows and outcome definitions, including binary conflict incidence, fatality counts, and 6- and 12-month forward windows. The one exception is a log-log TWFE OLS specification, which yields a positive and significant elasticity of $0.175$ ($p = 0.022$): a 1\% increase in predicted yield is associated with a 0.175\% increase in log conflict events. This finding is more consistent with a predation or resource-competition mechanism than an opportunity-cost channel, echoing results from \citet{DubeVargas2013}, who find that commodity price booms---rather than busts---increase conflict in Colombian regions with high resource appropriability.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it provides one of the first large-scale panel tests of the yield--conflict relationship that uses direct ML-predicted measures of crop productivity rather than climate proxies, allowing sharper identification of the agricultural channel. Second, it constructs and analyses a new multi-country, subnational panel covering 926 Admin-2 districts across six heterogeneous Sub-Saharan African countries over 15 years. Third, it provides an honest assessment of the limits of current satellite-based yield prediction methods, documenting near-zero predictive accuracy for Nigeria and Uganda and absent precipitation data as important constraints on inference.
