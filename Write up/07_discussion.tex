\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Interpreting the Null Result}

The main finding of this paper is that predicted maize yield has no statistically significant effect on the 3-month forward conflict count in the preferred TWFE OLS and Poisson count-model specifications. This null result is robust across alternative conflict windows (6 and 12 months), alternative outcome definitions (binary incidence, fatality count), and across the diverse set of countries and years in the panel. Several interpretations of this null deserve consideration.

First, the null may reflect the theoretical ambiguity discussed in Section~\ref{sec:theory}: the opportunity-cost and predation channels operate simultaneously and in opposite directions, generating a net effect close to zero at the average. If higher yields reduce conflict among some groups (those with comparative advantage in farming) while increasing it among others (armed groups competing for appropriable surplus), the aggregate effect at the district level may be statistically indistinguishable from zero. This interpretation is consistent with the literature on heterogeneous effects and with the evidence in \citet{DalBoDalBo2011} that the direction of the income--conflict relationship depends on the nature of the income shock.

Second, the null may partly reflect measurement limitations. For Nigeria and Uganda, where yield model spatial $R^2$ is near zero (0.018 and 0.016, respectively), the predicted yield measure contains very little signal about actual agricultural productivity and is dominated by noise. These two countries contribute 9,315 of the 13,890 panel observations (67\%), meaning that the full-panel estimates are heavily influenced by two countries where the key regressor has near-zero predictive validity. Restricting the sample to countries with higher model performance (Ethiopia, Malawi, Mali) would reduce the panel to 2,115 observations and introduce sample selection concerns, but suggests that measurement error attenuation bias is a plausible explanation for the null in the full panel.

Third, the forward conflict window design may be misaligned with the actual temporal dynamics of the yield--conflict relationship. If the primary channel operates through longer-run economic stress---chronic food insecurity, asset depletion, seasonal migration, and social breakdown---rather than immediate post-harvest opportunism, a 3-month window may be too short to capture the mechanism. The positive (though insignificant) trend in point estimates as the window lengthens (3-month: $\hat{\beta} = 2.386$; 12-month: $\hat{\beta} = 17.939$ in OLS) is suggestive in this direction, though sample power declines as the window extends due to the accumulation of confounders.

\subsection{Interpreting the Log-Log Elasticity}

The positive and significant elasticity from the log-log TWFE OLS specification ($\hat{\beta} = 0.175$, $p = 0.022$) is the most economically interpretable result in the paper and requires careful attention. A 1\% increase in predicted yield is associated with a 0.175\% increase in log conflict events in the subsequent 3 months. This positive elasticity is difficult to reconcile with the opportunity-cost channel, which predicts that better yields should reduce conflict by raising the return to farming. It is, however, consistent with the predation and resource-contestation channel: a better harvest creates more appropriable surplus, attracting the attention of armed groups and increasing the likelihood of violent appropriation.

This interpretation echoes the findings of \citet{DubeVargas2013} in the Colombian context, where positive commodity shocks to easily appropriable resources (oil) increased conflict while positive shocks to labour-intensive crops (coffee) reduced it. Maize in Sub-Saharan Africa occupies an intermediate position: it is a widely grown staple crop, but in some production systems (particularly large-scale or semi-commercial production) it can represent a contestable surplus that is accessible to armed groups through looting, taxation at markets, or territorial control of productive areas.

The magnitudes of the log-log effect, while statistically significant, are modest. A 10\% increase in predicted yield---a meaningful agricultural improvement---would be associated with only a 1.75\% increase in log conflict. Given that mean conflict events per district-year are 6.3, this corresponds to an increase of roughly 0.11 events---a small effect that is unlikely to be policy-relevant in most settings.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several important limitations should inform the interpretation of these results.

\textit{Yield model performance.} The XGBoost models achieve moderate predictive accuracy for Malawi (spatial $R^2 = 0.178$) and Mali ($R^2 = 0.192$), but near-zero accuracy for Uganda and Nigeria. The absence of precipitation data---the primary driver of yield variation in rain-fed systems---is a central constraint that is not remediable within the current data architecture. Future work incorporating satellite-derived precipitation (e.g.\ CHIRPS or GPM) could substantially improve predictive accuracy and reduce attenuation bias.

\textit{Crop coverage.} The yield models are trained and validated using maize as the primary crop. While maize is the dominant staple crop in most of the countries studied, other crops (sorghum, millet, cassava, rice) are important in some regions and may exhibit different relationships with conflict. The predicted yield measure therefore captures only one dimension of agricultural productivity.

\textit{Causal interpretation.} While the use of predicted rather than observed yields mitigates the most direct form of reverse causality, it does not eliminate all identification concerns. If conflict affects the satellite vegetation signal at the scale of Admin-2 districts---for example, through abandonment of farmland, burning of crops, or displacement of farming populations---then the predicted yield itself could be partially endogenous to conflict. Given the coarseness of the satellite signal and the localised nature of most conflict events relative to district area, this concern is likely small but not fully dismissible.

\textit{IV weakness.} The failure of the 2SLS approach to yield a valid causal estimate is a substantive limitation. The sparse overlap between LSMS survey coverage and the panel period means that district-year mean observed yields are measured with large sampling error, attenuating the first-stage relationship below the threshold of instrument relevance. Researchers seeking to replicate or extend this analysis would benefit from panel surveys with higher temporal frequency, such as longitudinal household surveys or administrative yield records.
